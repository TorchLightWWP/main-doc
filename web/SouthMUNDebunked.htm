SouthMUN Debunked

How mocks work

Mocks are simple, at least in concept. You do research on whatever topic the officers have given you, you attend the thing for 8+ hours and attempt to impress the officers enough to get selected to go a conference, whether it’s a trainer or a national.

 

The reality of prepping for a mock and actually getting INTO a conference, however, is a bit more complex than what I just laid out. The key to making a MUN conference is to speak well, lead well, and write well, most likely in that order. Speaking and leading are given close to equal amounts of weight when it comes to making decisions, with writing coming in the not-so-important third, these mocks last a ridiculous amount of time. Having a really killer speech or demonstrating consistent leadership will probably get you into a conference. Arguably, having great speeches will help even more so, only because speeches become really dry about four hours in and anyone who can break the curse of awful speeches is a winner in everyone’s eyes. So just be smart about what you do and play to your strengths, whether that’s speaking, leading, or both (preferably both). Also, don’t sit around, because you never know what the officers will be paying attention to.

 

 

What the conferences are

Conferences are essentially glossier versions of mocks that last four days, throw you into a shark tank with around one hundred or so other competitors who may or may not hate your guts, and expect you to come out as the winner.

 

Conferences are expanded versions of mocks, but more fun and more work. There’s lots of late nights, lots of stress, and lots of truly terrifying competition, but there’s also tons of great team bonding and networking opportunities with kids who are every bit as ambitious and driven as you are.

 

Also, while individual awards do matter, it’s the overall team performance that matters most. The team needs to have enough individual award winners to qualify for a team award (i.e. Best Small Delegation) to keep its place as the top public school team in the nation.

Recommendations on how to be an officer

1.    Go to every conference financially possible

2.    Win high-ranking awards consistently or at least show a positive growth curve

3.   Don’t just rely on you partner - show that you can pull for the team by yourself, too.

4.    Perform well at mocks.

5.    Ask officers for help and feedback! Improves your visibility and shows you care

6.    Don’t be a mean! Also shows that you care about the club

7.    Don’t do anything illegal or break any rules at conferences/in school. Ruins your reputation for life.

8.    Don’t pander to the leadership unnecessarily – they’ll guess your ulterior motives.

9.    Run for officer.

10. Win!

11. Work very, very hard.
