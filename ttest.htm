Dear Writing Faculty,

           

Thank you. Throughout my schooling, I was simply told to ‘add evidence’ or ‘be more persuasive’ without being shown how. Somehow, experts on communication, could not communicate. However, here, my questions have been answered. Consequently, I have grown as a critical writer and am ready to face my undergraduate writing career.

I am most indebted, perhaps, for being shown how to rigorously identify and articulate the elements of reasoning. I learned the first two elements, reasons and evidence, from our discussion of D. Farris’ article. Since then, I have internalized the difference between the two: a reason is a proposition that, if shown, implies the larger proposition whereas evidence is an instance of a reason occurring in real life. Now I understand how to answer ‘why?’ and how providing an example in place of a reason does not do so. Understanding the last and paramount element of reasoning, analysis, was more challenging.  From high school, I thought analysis was merely a breaking into parts. Until we clearly defined propositions and premises in class, I was a pencil without paper. After those definitions sunk in, I became aware that some statements are accepted by a reader and others need to use the former statements to be shown. Subsequently, I see analysis is connecting a proposition to a chain of reasons, the last a premise. The breaking into parts occurs between links, describing the parts of a former statement in a manner that implies the latter. With this knowledge, I can pinpoint errors and articulate solutions. Now, it is upon this sense of, this feel for propositions, premises, reasons, and evidence that I compose; I command my reasoning. 

This control is evident in my research paper. To show my proposition that Archilochus’ cologne epode includes the purpose of depicting the speaker’s emotional resolution, I show that the speaker hates seeing himself as hasty. This required nuance in distinguishing the speaker’s words as evidence and a statement about the speaker as a reason. So, with critical thought, I wrote down my reason: the speaker hated viewing himself as hasty because he was excessive in his attack on Neobule. From this, I claimed he was not trying to be rhetorical. Then, I concluded the excessiveness was emotional, an outburst of anger. Finally, I illustrated my point by stating the premise: hating another for being a certain way implies one would hate being that way. I ensured my writing was grounded through this process of outlining my analysis.

This experience was unlike anything I had done in high school. Back then, I only followed examples: writing mimicking that which had succeeded before or was presented in class. Presently, I follow reasons.  Observe.

 

 

Graciously yours,

 

Divesh Otwani

More than Pleasure: Archilochus’ Cologne Epode

 

Much is confusing about the purpose of Archilochus’s cologne epode. Many scholars see it as dramatic iambic entertainment. They note the speaker’s rhetorical skill in maneuvering through the maiden’s worries and suggest the purpose is to depict this art of seduction. Other scholars are convinced that the epode is an attack at the Lycambids: the younger daughter shamed by deflowering and the older shamed through verbal attack. Some scholars see a mix. Regardless, a view on the speaker’s emotions emerges. Both seem to think the speaker is simply after pleasure and that the poet either uses this to shame the Lycambids or depict a masculine show of the art of seduction (or some mixture). Here, I note a third understanding of the speaker’s emotions[1]: he despises the self-image that he is hasty and reckless like Neobule. Using this understanding, I will show the purpose of the poem includes a depiction of the speaker resolving his emotions by eradicating this self-image.

The speaker’s hatred of viewing himself as hasty like Neobule is evident through the excessive emotion displayed in his attack on Neobule. For instance, the speaker gets so riled up at one point that he exclaims “Go to hell! May [Zeus] not send this upon me so that I… will be a source of shame to my neighbors.”[2] He is so angry that he exclaims in apostrophe, imagining he is speaking directly to Neobule. Further, he calls out to the mighty Zeus to not send ‘this’ (Neobule) on him as though ‘this’ was some tragedy like the death of a family member. The image is of him yelling out to Neobule and Zeus while directly in front of the maiden. Such anger is not seductive (yelling ‘go to hell’) and thus, not some rhetorical means of convincing the girl. This is an emotional outburst of hating how ‘easy’ and wanton Neobule is. One can imagine how upset the speaker would be at the thought of seeing himself with this characteristic he has denounced. Rubin[3] argues this point thoroughly. She shows the attack was an “outlet for [the speaker’s] emotional excess” by looking at how harshly the speaker contrasts the two potential relationships[4].

The speaker’s loathing at seeing himself as hasty is also evident through the rhetorical context of the invective: the speaker’s attempt to demonstrate to the maiden that he is not hasty and a fit for Neobule. The maiden accuses the speaker of being hasty, uncontrolled and a match for Neobule and he responds to this with his attack on Neobule. She says, if, instead of waiting until after marriage to be with her, his “heart is pressing right through” him, he can go to a paradoxically ‘delicate, beautiful maiden’ who really wants relations[5]. She taunts the speaker by suggesting that he should go to the undesirable, wanton Neobule- a perfect match for his present haste. In his response, he presents three points[6] before the attack on Neobule: 1) they can do some lesser pleasure of Aphrodite, 2) they can have full coitus[7] much later (presumably after marriage)[8] and, 3) he will be contraceptive and do coitus interruptus[9]. All this sounds perfect, not at all hasty, and completely agreeable but, the speaker knows that the maiden is skeptical that he is just saying anything he can to trick her and have full coitus. What is missing is the proof that he is what he says he is. Thus, he attacks Neobule for her hastiness to show that he truly does hate uncontrolled behavior and will not behave that way himself[10]. Now, that the speaker gets emotional and angry at Neobule for her haste while trying to show that he is not hasty suggests that he truly loathes thinking of himself as hasty. The speaker is defending himself and in doing so gets passionate (and unrhetorical as seen by his yelling) because he really despises the self-view the maiden has suggested of him.

            The speaker’s discomfort at him being a reckless, hasty person clarifies a few central aspects of the epode. First, the ending in which the speaker ‘comes into shore at grassy gardens’ is resolved. His motivation for his action is unclear. If the epode’s primary purpose is to shame the Lycambids, why is the speaker treating the maiden respectfully by keeping his promise?  On the other hand, if the epode is more of a depiction of the heroic wooing of a girl, then why does the speaker restrain himself from taking as much pleasure as he can? In any case, the speaker restrains himself from the “divine”[11] pleasure when one would expect him to do otherwise. [12] However, if the speaker were to break his promise, he would be like Neobule- hasty and “two-faced.”[13] Because the speaker is disgusted by this idea, despite all the tension of the moment, he keeps his promise and proves to himself that he is not like Neobule and has restraint. Similarly, the speaker is deeply affirmed when he convinces the maiden, who at the start of the epode, thought he was hasty. The maiden, by the end, is revealing her own skin and trusting the speaker[14]. In light of this disgust the speaker has for seeing himself as hasty, this trust by someone who was unsure at first is deeply affirming; the self-view that he is hasty has been destroyed because she who presented the view has been convinced. This gives new meaning to the trust the iambic seducer typically wishes to gain. Here, unlike the traditional seduction, the speaker cares that the girl believes him not because it will allow him to have relations, but because he wants to be good.

            These two affirmations in the ending demonstrate the poem’s purpose includes a depiction of the speaker’s emotional resolution; the speaker tries to eradicate the perception of him as thoughtless and hasty by convincing this maiden and then keeping his promise. However, this begs the question of why Archilochus would include this purpose as part of his poem. Perhaps the speaker’s discomfort is Archilochus’. This would suggest that Archilochus gives more than a simple depiction of seduction, but rather real comments on good and bad relationships, good and bad characteristics- both from experience of some Neobule[15]. In brief, perhaps Archilochus’s pain lends not simply a seductive tale, but wisdom on what is and what looks like positive love.

[2] This and all my other textual use is from the Lavigne translation. I agree with his interpretation and so, use it to make my case. I have attached the translation at the end for reference.

[3]To Rubin’s argument, I merely add a few examples and use her points in conjunction with the rhetorical context to show the speaker’s emotions. Moreover, my argument derives its inspiration from her article; when I considered the poem through the lens of the speaker emotions, I was able to consider that the purpose of the poem might include the resolution of the speaker’s emotions.

[4] Rubin frames the speaker’s situation as having the possibility of two relationships and denying one through “a vehement and abusive attack on Neobule’s character” which “may correlate with a former excessive passion”.  Rubin observes the speaker fears “I may bring forth dark and sinful things just as a bitch, swiftly being aroused” (pg. 138-39). She explains that the speaker actually fears a renewed relationship with Neobule or one like it because being hasty and choosing Neobule would ‘equate him with whom he has denounced as the worst’ – something he would hate. So, the speaker hates this image of himself as the type of person who is a match for Neobule and denounces this possible relationship.

[5]  First, Van Sickle has shown that because the girl chooses to stay and reason with the speaker (despite the obvious risk of rape in that time) and she proposes that they ‘endure’ until marriage, she is interested in the speaker. Then, on her suggesting Neobule, Van Sickle notes the idea of an eager woman is “at odds” with the “image of a” beautiful, soft girl. He shows the girl is ‘pushing unsuitable merchandise’ (pg. 5-6). I disagree on his explanation for why and instead agree with Eckerman’s view: “flirtation is a game.” Eckerman suggests the maiden playfully “points to the impropriety of the male’s advances” by proposing a hasty match for him (pg. 11-13). However, I disagree with Eckerman’s use of this observed taunting for the purpose of arguing that the offered girl was a slave girl. Eckerman relies on the ambiguity of ‘in my house’ to make his case. The phrase ‘in my house’ is ambiguous because the audience was aware of the Archilochean myth or stock characters which meant the reference was obviously to Neobule.

[6] I borrow these three points from the analysis of the speaker’s response by Lavigne (pg. 42-46). With these, I show that what is lacking after these points is a demonstration of the speaker’s truthfulness. 

[7] Full coitus is regular intercourse whereas coitus interruptus is commonly known as ‘pulling out’.

[8] Line 11. I agree with Lavigne’s interpretation (pg. 43) that the speaker promises to have full coitus with the girl only when he is much older, when he has grown a beard/when his “cheek is blackened.”

[9]Line 15. I agree with the interpretation that keeping to ‘grassy gardens’ refers to pubic hair- the precise location that the speaker will ejaculate. However, this is debated and I address the other views shortly.

[10] I agree with Rubin that the attack “offers a negative exemplum of excess for the narrator who separates or isolates himself from Neobule’s excesses” (pg. 140). I demonstrate this idea differently by analyzing the points made by the speaker and the elephant in the room- that he could just be lying to get what he wants (and thus he provides evidence of why he hates hastiness).  

[11] Line 10, Lavigne translation. The ‘divine thing’ refers to full coitus.

[12] Rankin and Henderson see the speaker as a “detached sensualist” (Rankin 607) trying to achieve full coitus “by whatever means necessary” (Henderson 169). Henderson uses this as a reason to argue the ending is full coitus. Regardless, the speaker keeping his promise is unexpected and brings up the question of his motivation. West and Eckerman view the speaker as an “enthralled lover” who is depicted in a “sexual adventure” to an iambic audience (West 218-19). In this case as well, coitus interruptus seems out of character, “too unheroic” to fit a theme of “sexual conquest” (Eckerman 17). I present a strong emotion to explain this highly unusual action: the speaker’s hatred of seeing himself like Neobule. Further, Henderson’s explanation of full coitus is quite perplexing in light of this emotion.

[13] Line 24, Lavigne translation. A promise breaker is “two-faced.”

[14] Line 33, Lavigne translation. “She, then, was exposing her fresh skin” is a controversial translation that I agree with. Note that this view breaks any notion of the act not being consensual.  

[15] I write ‘some Neobule’ because there does not need to be a real Neobule for Archilochus to be talking about a real girl. Even if he uses a stock character to express some idea, it still comes from his experience with some girl.
